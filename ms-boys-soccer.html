Pre-Season · Week 1 of 3

Foundation Week

Establish culture, learn each other, and build the basics. Everyone starts fresh here — that's the point. High energy, short lines, lots of touches.

🏅 How Skill Differentiation Works

  • ◉ Beginner track — simplified version of every drill, more reps, more coaching attention
  • ◉ Advanced track — added constraints (fewer touches, speed, pressure, weak foot)
  • Small-sided games mix skill levels intentionally — more-experienced players must make teammates better

Every Session Structure

  • 0–5 min — Team Huddle: energy-setter, verse
  • 5–15 min — Dynamic Warm-Up (ball at feet)
  • 15–35 min — Technical Focus (differentiated by level) — trim this first if time is short
  • 35–55 min — Competitive Game (mixed teams) — protect this block, it's where the learning sticks
  • 55–60 min — Cool-Down + Devotional Moment

Game Time Is the Priority, Not the Reward

  • Isolated drills teach a technique. Small-sided games teach a player when and why to use it under real pressure — that's where it actually sticks.
  • If a technical block runs long, cut it short. Never cut the scrimmage short to finish a drill.
  • Every drill block below is written with a target time — treat it as a ceiling, not a floor. If the team gets it in 10 minutes instead of 20, move to the game early.
  • When in doubt, default to more 3v3/4v4 game time and less standing-in-line drilling, especially in Weeks 2–3 and all of in-season.
Session 1 First Touch & Passing
Day 1 First Touch + Passing Gates ▾
0–5
Opening Huddle
Bring it in tight. Introduce yourself and the team's 5 values: discipline, perseverance, sportsmanship, self-sacrifice, teamwork. Read 2 Timothy 2:4–5. Ask: "What does it mean to compete by the rules, for the One who enlisted you?" Take 2 quick answers. End with hands in — "Warriors on 3."
Set the tone: this team works hard because of who we represent, not just what we want to win.
5–15
Warm-Up: Dribble Maze Everyone
Every player has a ball in a 20×20 grid. Coach calls commands: Red light (sole stop), Green light (dribble), Blue (inside turn), Yellow (outside chop). Gradually increase speed. Ends with 1 minute of freestyle — show your best move.
This tells you everything. Watch who has natural comfort with the ball. Note the beginners — they'll need you near them in the next block.
15–35
Passing Gates (Differentiated)
Scatter 10 cone gates (2 cones, 2 yds apart) across the field. Pairs pass through as many gates as possible in 4 minutes. Track completions. Then beat your score.

◉ Beginner◉ Advanced
Distance5–8 yd passes, wide gates10–15 yds, narrow gates
Touch ruleUnlimited touches2 touches max (receive + pass)
FootDominant foot fineMust alternate feet each pass
ChallengeBeat your last scoreOne-touch through 3 gates in a row
Pair one beginner with one more experienced player. The experienced player practices leadership + weak foot. The beginner gets a mentor, not a lecture.
~8
First Touch Under Pressure (Differentiated)
Pairs, 5 yds apart. Server passes firm — not soft — and receiver must control it away from their feet into open space (not straight back to where it came from) before passing back. This is the single biggest separator between a chaotic team and a composed one. Don't skip it just because it looks simple.

◉ Beginner◉ Advanced
Pass speedModerate, rolling passFirm, driven pass
Touch2 touches allowed (control, then pass)1 touch to control and set, 1 to pass — no more
Add-onNone yet — just get a clean touchReceiver must check shoulder before the ball arrives (look over shoulder for "pressure")
Coach the habit, not just the technique: "Cushion it away from pressure, not back into it." Say this phrase all season — it becomes instinct by mid-season.
35–40
Rondo: 4v1 Everyone
Groups of 5 in a circle (~8 yds). 4 keep possession from 1 defender. Last passer when defender wins it goes in the middle. Played for 5 minutes. Great equalizer — even beginners can succeed with positioning.
Coach beginners to "show for the ball" (open body, in space). They don't need skill yet — they need positioning habits.
40–55
4v4 Passing Game Mixed Teams
30×20 yd grid. Score by passing through a cone gate (no shooting). Must complete 3 passes before scoring. Mix skill levels on each team. Rotate teams every 6 minutes.
Advanced add-on: If your team completes 5 passes in a row, next gate counts as 2 points. Encourages patience even for the strongest players.
55–60
Cool-Down Circle
Light quad and hamstring stretch. Go around the circle: each player says their name and one word for how they feel. Coach closes: "I saw effort from everyone today. That's what we're about." Optional: 10-second moment of silence as a team to close it out.
Day 2 Dribbling & 1v1 Moves ▾
0–5
Opening Huddle
Quick accountability: "Who practiced juggling since yesterday?" (No shame if no one did — just plant the seed.) High energy — clap it up, get loud. Today's verse: Proverbs 27:17 — "As iron sharpens iron, so one person sharpens another." Frame: "Today we push each other to be better."
5–15
Warm-Up: Cone Slalom + Juggling Station Split
Half the team does a slalom course through 6 cones (20 yds), time themselves, try to beat their time. Other half does juggle challenge (count their best). Swap after 5 mins. Every player gets a number — encourage them to write it down and beat it by end of season.
15–35
Move of the Day: The Cut-Back Turn (Differentiated)
Everyone learns the same move: dribble toward a cone, inside foot chop back, accelerate away. Walk-through → slow → full speed. Then line drill attacking a standing coach.

◉ Beginner◉ Advanced
ApproachSlow dribble, wide setupSprint approach, tight space
DefenderCone only (stationary)Live defender (light pressure)
FinishNo finish required — just the turnTurn + shot on goal immediately
BonusTry with non-dominant foot onceMust finish with non-dominant foot
For beginners: slow it down. The move feels unnatural until it suddenly clicks. Celebrate that click loudly when it happens.
35–45
King of the Square Everyone
10×10 yd grids. Pairs: attacker tries to dribble across the defender's end line; defender tries to knock the ball out. 45 seconds per matchup. Rotate winners to new grids. Run simultaneously — everyone is playing at the same time, no standing in line.
Beginner tweak: Defender can only shuffle side-to-side (no tackling yet). Builds attacker confidence first.
45–55
5v5 Dribble-to-Score Game Mixed Teams
Goals replaced by end lines — score by dribbling over the line (no shooting). Rewards ball carriers. Advanced players challenged to use the cut-back in game; beginners just encouraged to drive forward with confidence.
55–60
Debrief + Verse Response
Calf stretch. "Who sharpened someone today?" — call out a teammate who made you better. Quick moment. Coach affirms: "That's a team. Let's go." Hands in, break it down.
Iron sharpens iron — the strongest players lifting everyone else is a kingdom principle, not just a soccer one.
Day 3 Shooting & Finishing ▾
0–5
Opening Huddle
Energy check: "0–10, what's the team's intensity right now?" Whatever they say — challenge them to make it a 10 by the end. Verse: Philippians 4:13. "Everything we do today — with Him."
5–15
Pass-and-Move Warm-Up
Two lines at midfield cones. Pass across, follow your pass. After 3 minutes add: sprint to a wide cone after passing, then rejoin opposite line. Gets blood flowing with a ball involved.
15–35
Shooting Circuit (3 Stations, 5 min each)
Quick technique reminder: plant foot beside ball, strike with laces, follow through low. "Pick a corner — always have a target."

Station◉ Beginner◉ Advanced
① Straight shot12 yds, stationary ball, focus on technique18–20 yds, driven shot, pick corner
② Rolling ballCoach rolls slowly, player approaches and shootsFirst-time finish, no second touch allowed
③ Turn & shootReceive to feet, take a touch, then shootBack to goal, receive, spin, finish in one motion
Beginners: repetition over difficulty. 3 successful techniques beat 1 flashy attempt. Get the basics grooved.
35–45
3v1 into Goal Mixed
3 attackers (1 advanced + 2 beginners or all mixed) vs 1 defender + GK. Start at midfield. Emphasis: advanced player sets teammates up — not a solo show. Award bonus point if a beginner scores.
The advanced player being a facilitator teaches them the most valuable skill at this level: making others better. Coach it explicitly: "You're the engine. They're the finishers today."
45–55
First Full Scrimmage
Let them play. Minimal coaching. Watch. Take mental notes on natural positions, competitiveness, attitude. Who encourages? Who gets frustrated? Who tracks back? You're reading the team, not correcting it yet.
55–60
End of Week 1 Moment
Team circle. "What's one thing God is teaching you through being on this team?" (No pressure — just offer it.) Coach shares something too. High-five line on the way out.
End-of-week moments build a culture that outlasts the season. Even the players who don't engage much are watching how this goes.
Pre-Season · Week 2 of 3

Defense, Shape & Transition

Now that everyone has had touches on the ball, teach the team how to win it back and how to strike fast when they do. Defense is how you compete — even with beginners.

⚡ Week 2 Focus: Competitive Edge

  • Beginners: learn where to be. Positioning is coachable — skill takes time.
  • Advanced: lead by example under pressure. Add constraints to keep them challenged.
  • Theme verse for the week: "Be strong and courageous." — Joshua 1:9
Session 4 Defensive Stance & Pressure
Day 4 Defending: Delay, Don't Dive ▾
0–5
Opening Huddle
Joshua 1:9 — "Be strong and courageous." Frame it: "Defending takes courage. You're going to stand your ground today. That's not natural — but it's how good teams win." Hands in to open.
5–15
Shadow Defensive Footwork
Pairs. One dribbles slowly. Partner stays in low defensive stance, 1 yd in front, mirroring. No tackling — just stay in front. Switch every 90 sec. Focus: toes, knees bent, goalside, eyes on the ball. Do it 3 rounds, then add: defender tries to force attacker left (their weak side).
For beginners: this is their moment. Defending is about positioning, not athleticism. They can be great at this fast.
15–35
1v1 Defending Drill (Differentiated)
Attacker starts 20 yds from goal, defender 5 yds away. Defender's only job: slow the attack, stay on feet, force to the weak side. Not to win the ball. 10 reps per pair.

◉ Beginner Defender◉ Advanced Defender
GoalStay in front for 5 secondsWin the ball cleanly without fouling
Attacker speedWalk/jog paceFull speed
SuccessNo lunge = successForce to weak side AND win ball
Loudly praise the patient defender. "That's exactly it — he made it hard without diving. That wins games."
35–45
Team Shape Walk-Through (No Ball)
Put everyone in your basic defensive formation. Walk through: pressure, cover, balance. Move them around by calling "ball goes left"... "ball switches"... "cross comes in." Like a chess drill. Do it 3× from different starting points. It will feel slow — that's fine. Muscle memory starts here.
45–55
5v5 Defensive Points Game
Score system: 1 pt for tackling and keeping possession, 2 pts for a GK save + distribution, 3 pts for a clean 2-minute defensive period, 1 pt for a goal. Changes the mindset from goals-only to total performance.
55–60
Cool-Down
Glute stretch, quad. Discuss: "What's the difference between courage and recklessness in defending?" One or two answers. Short prayer of thanks for the practice, then break it down.
Day 5 Transition & Counterattack ▾
0–5
Opening Huddle
"How many goals are scored within 10 seconds of winning the ball? Most of them." Today is about being dangerous the moment you get possession. Hustle culture starts NOW.
5–15
Agility Warm-Up + Sprint Bursts
Cone patterns (or ladder if available): 2-feet-in, lateral shuffle, in-out. Then 4× 20 yd sprint at 80% — recovery walk back. Fast session needs primed legs.
15–35
Transition Game: Win It, Use It
3v3 in 25×20 grid, two small goals. Rule: when possession changes, the new team must get a shot within 6 seconds or possession resets. Teaches immediate forward thinking.

◉ Beginner◉ Advanced
Time limit8 seconds to shoot4 seconds to shoot
First actionJust advance the ball forwardMust switch direction before shooting
Advanced players: remind them the constraint exists so they can't just overpower beginners. They must be faster and smarter.
35–45
3v2 Counterattack Waves
3 attackers at midfield, 2 defenders at the 18. Coach passes, attackers go immediately. Emphasize: wide players stay wide, ball carrier commits the defender, pass early. After each rep, 2 attackers become defenders for the next wave.
Role assignment: Put beginners as wide runners — gives them clear, achievable roles (stay wide, run the channel). Advanced player plays #9, making decisions under pressure.
45–55
Scrimmage — Transition Focus
Normal scrimmage. Coach stops play maximum 3 times — only to highlight transition moments (good or missed). Keep the flow. Let them feel the speed.
55–60
Cool-Down
Hip flexors, calves. One word each: how did the team's hustle feel? Coach response to what they say. Break it down.
Day 6 Set Pieces + Team Identity ▾
0–5
Opening Huddle — Team Roles
Announce starting to think about positions. Ask players what position they want to play and why. Make notes. Verse: Romans 12:4–5 — "Just as each of us has one body with many members, and these members do not all have the same function..." Frame it: "Every role matters. The team needs everyone."
5–15
Warm-Up Rondo 5v2
5 keep-away vs 2 in the middle in a 12-yd circle. Two-touch max. Defenders win a point for every ball won in under 8 passes. Run 3 rounds of 2 minutes each. High energy.
15–30
Corner Kicks — 2 Plays
Play 1 (Near-post flick): One runner attacks near post, everyone else creates space. Simple. Put advanced player as corner taker.

Play 2 (Short corner): Ball to player at edge of box, cross from wider angle. Beginners learn runner routes; advanced learn delivery.

5 reps each, both sides. Defenders set up to create realistic pressure.
Keep it 2 plays. At this age, 5 plays = 0 plays. Two they own beats five they've heard.
30–42
Free Kicks + Wall Practice
Kicks from 20–25 yds. Teach wall setup: 3–4 players, GK covers far post. Rotate who takes kicks — don't only give it to the advanced players. Beginners get reps too, even if the execution isn't perfect. After 6 attempts, practice crossing free kicks with runners attacking.
Beginners taking free kicks in practice = confidence in games. The skill grows if they get reps.
42–47
Throw-In Routine — 2 Options
Throw-ins happen constantly and almost never get practiced — fix that here. Teach 2 simple options: ① Down the line — throw to a winger checking to the ball, who then plays it forward into space; ② Flick-on — throw to a player with their back to goal near midfield, who flicks it first-time into space behind the defense for a runner. Run each option 4× from both sidelines. Remind throwers: both feet on the ground, ball behind the head, no foul throws.
Two options, same as corners. Don't add more — a throw-in taker who has to think too hard just throws it straight to the nearest defender.
47–55
Scrimmage w/ Set Piece Triggers
When a corner, free kick, or throw-in is earned, pause and run the actual play. Real-game reps in context.
55–60
End of Week 2 + Captain Vote
Stretch circle. Quietly hold up fingers to vote for team captain(s). Announce result. Let captain say something. Close with a prayer led by coach or volunteer player.
Shared prayer, even brief, builds the kind of trust teams need to compete together under pressure.
Pre-Season · Week 3 of 3

Game-Ready Week

Put everything together. Simulate real game scenarios, establish your lineup, and build confidence heading into the season. Game week starts now.

🎯 Week 3 Goals

  • Beginners: know their position, know 1 set piece role, feel confident enough to compete
  • Advanced: lead on the field, communicate, take responsibility
  • Team: know your 3 principles. Run your 2 set pieces. Trust each other.
Session 7 Possession + Shape Under Pressure
Day 7 Possession & Playing Out of the Back ▾
0–5
Opening Huddle
"Week 3. Game week is coming. Who's ready?" High energy call-and-response. Verse: 2 Timothy 1:7 — "For God has not given us a spirit of fear, but of power, love, and self-discipline." "No panic-kicking. No fear. Today we play brave soccer."
5–15
Rondo 6v2 — Possession Warm-Up
6 keep-away vs 2 in 15×15 grid. Team of 6 earns a point every 10 consecutive passes. Two-touch limit for all. Changes every 3 minutes. Gets everyone's head up and passing sharp.
15–35
Building Out of the Back (Differentiated)
Half-field. GK + 3 defenders build against 2 high-press attackers. Goal: play the ball past the midfield line without hoofing it.

◉ Beginner Defenders◉ Advanced Defenders
Press speedAttackers press at 50% speedFull press, game speed
SuccessBall reaches midfield any wayMust maintain possession through midfield
GKCan hold 5 seconds before distributingMust distribute in 3 seconds
Praise the brave short pass under pressure every time. That's the behavior that wins games when it's 1–1 in the second half.
35–55
Full 7v7 Match Simulation
Use your formation and actual positions. Coach from the sideline only — no stepping onto the field. Allow 4–5 stoppages maximum. Focus stoppages only on your 3 team principles. Let them problem-solve in real time.
After each stoppage ask the players first: "What should we have done there?" Then reinforce the right answer.
55–60
Pre-Game Culture Setup
"What are our 5 team values?" — players say them (discipline, perseverance, sportsmanship, self-sacrifice, teamwork). "What are our 3 team principles on the field?" — players say them. Write them somewhere visible. Ownership is everything.
Day 8 GK + Defensive Shape + Full Scrimmage ▾
0–5
Opening Huddle
Captain leads the huddle today. Coach steps back. Verse is captain's choice (gave them one to prep: Psalm 133:1 — "How good and pleasant it is when brothers dwell in unity"). Captain gives 1 minute of fire. You just watch.
Transferring leadership to the captain builds team ownership. The captain needs reps leading before game day.
5–15
Split: GK Work + Team Defensive Shape
GK (with assistant or coach helper): Diving saves low both sides (5 each), distribution (punt, throw, goal kick), command of cross. 2 players rotate through GK reps for development.

Outfield (you): 6 players in defensive shape, no ball. Call out "ball left," "switch," "cross coming" — unit shifts together. Walk-through → game pace. 3 rounds.
15–35
Compact Defending vs Attack (6v6)
6 vs 6 on a half field (sized for your typical practice turnout). Defending team keeps shape and defends deep — goal is to not concede for 4 minutes. Attacking team gets possession and must break down a set block. Swap roles every 4 min. Advanced players play in key defensive roles; beginners fill in a set position and hold it. If you have extras, rotate them in every 4 minutes too.
35–55
Full Coached Scrimmage
Last big scrimmage before game week. 4–5 coaching stoppages max. Focus only on your top 2 corrections — not everything. Let them find solutions. GK rotates at halftime.
55–60
Every Player Shout-Out
Go around the circle — coach calls each player's name and says one specific strength they showed in the last 2 weeks. Every. Single. Player. Prepare this in advance. Don't wing it. This is powerful for the kids who aren't the stars.
Every player being seen and called by name — that's more than a coaching technique. It's ministry.
Day 9 Final Prep — Light, Sharp & Ready ▾
0–5
Opening Huddle — Game Week
High energy. "We have been preparing. We are ready. Whatever happens in that first game, we compete with everything we have — for each other and for God." Verse: 2 Timothy 2:4–5 again — full circle to Day 1. Hands in.
5–15
Fun Warm-Up: Team Juggling World Cup
Everyone juggles. Drop = sit down (but keep counting from where you left off individually). Last one wins nothing — just pride. Coach tries too. Guaranteed laughs. Gets the energy right.
15–25
Set Piece Review — No Pressure
Walk through corners, free kicks, and your 2 throw-in options. 3 reps each. No coaching — just reinforcing memory. If they do it wrong, just say "again" calmly. They know it by now.
25–48
Light Possession + Penalty Shootout
Rondo to stay sharp (8 min). Then fun penalty shootout — whole team, first side to 5 wins. GK gets reps too. Keep it loose, keep it fun. Fresh legs > extra work the day before a game.
Don't overwork them. A sharp, confident team beats a tired, over-drilled one. Protect the legs here.
48–60
Game Day Logistics + Team Moment
Cover: arrival time, what to bring, warm-up routine, starting lineup (if ready). Share it confidently — "everyone will play." Then: a full team prayer. Coach leads it. Thank God for health, for this team, for the chance to compete. Ask for courage, teamwork, and to represent the school well. Close with hands in — make it the loudest one of pre-season.
The team that prays together before a game remembers what the day is really about. It doesn't guarantee a win — it guarantees perspective.
Phase 2 — Game Season

In-Season Sessions

1–2 games + 1–2 training sessions per week. Pick the right session type based on where you are in the week. The goal: arrive at every game fresh, focused, and unified.

In-Season Weekly Framework

  • Day after game: Light or rest. No heavy training. Optional optional recovery touches.
  • 2+ days before a game: Use Session A (Fix) or Session C (Develop). Work harder here.
  • Day before a game: Session B only. Light, sharp, confident. No heavy legs.
  • Game day: Use the game-day warm-up every time — routine builds readiness.
In-Season Sessions Pick Based on Week
Session A Post-Game Fix (Address One Issue) ▾
0–5
Opening Huddle — Reset
After a loss: acknowledge it once, genuinely. "We didn't get the result. Here's the one thing we're going to fix today." After a win: "Good. Now let's be better." Never skip the huddle. The tone you set here shapes the hour. Remember the standard: we play to win, but our preparation, effort, and attitude — not the scoreboard — are what we're actually judged on (see "Win Like a Warrior, Lose Like a Warrior" in the Culture & Faith tab).
How a team handles adversity is formed in these moments — not in the game. After a hard loss, a coach who stays steady and focused on growth teaches something that outlasts the season.
5–15
High-Energy Rondo Warm-Up
5v2, then 6v2. Fast pace. Gets energy up. No dwelling on the last game — get them moving and competing immediately.
15–40
The One Fix — Choose Based on Last Game
Problem: Gave up goals from wide areas
→ 2 defenders close down wide attacker, one blocks cross, one tracks runner. Repeat 10× each side. Include beginners as the tracking runner — clear role.

Problem: Can't hold possession
→ 4v4 possession in 20×20, no goals. 5 passes = 1 point. 2-touch max for advanced, unlimited for beginners. Teaches scanning and movement.

Problem: Not creating chances
→ Wall pass + combination drill. 4 attackers vs 2 defenders, finish on goal. Advanced players must play beginners in before shooting.

Problem: Getting outworked (fitness/intensity)
→ High-intensity 3v3 games, 90-second rounds, no rest between rounds. Winner stays. Competitive pressure = fitness.
Name the problem at the start. Name it clearly. "We gave up 3 goals from crosses. Today we fix that." Boys this age respond well to direct clarity — they want to know the problem is being taken seriously.
40–55
Free Scrimmage
No stops. Let them play. Watch for the fix being applied naturally. Reward it verbally in real time.
55–60
Wrap-Up
"Did anyone use what we drilled today?" Take 2–3 answers. Close with a quick verse or encouragement. Break it down.
Session B Pre-Game Prep (Day Before) ▾
0–5
Huddle — Game Eve Energy
Build anticipation. "Tomorrow is our chance." Share the lineup. Address the opponent briefly: "They're competitive. So are we. Here's what matters: our shape and our effort." Keep it 2 points max.
5–15
Light Dynamic Warm-Up
High knees, side shuffles, butt kicks, leg swings. Moderate pace. No intensity yet — this is activation, not conditioning.
15–25
Set Piece Refresher
Corners, free kicks, and throw-in options — 3 reps each, no pressure. Reinforce memory. Calm, confident repetition only.
25–50
Possession + Finishing — Light & Fun
Rondo to stay sharp (10 min). Then relaxed finishing — everyone takes 5 shots from their preferred spot. End with penalty competition: full squad, winner earns locker room bragging rights. Keep it FUN. Confidence goes into tomorrow's game.
Energy here should be 7/10. Not flat — but not gassed. The game tomorrow is what matters.
50–60
Game Plan + Team Prayer
Review: arrival time, warm-up plan, 2 tactical focuses. Captain speaks. Coach prays. Hands in to close.
Session C Midseason Development (Skill Growth) ▾

Use This When the Week Allows

  • This session works best when you have 2+ days before the next game and the team isn't worn down. If the schedule is heavy (2 games that week, short rest), skip the new skill and run Session A or B instead.
  • Don't force the skill-of-the-month onto a tired or game-heavy week just to stay on schedule. Possession, conditioning, and confidence take priority over adding something new.
0–5
Opening Huddle
Midseason check-in. "Where are we? Where do we want to be? Who has gotten better since Day 1?" Celebrate growth out loud. Verse: Hebrews 12:1 — "Run with perseverance the race set before us."
5–15
Technical Warm-Up + Juggle Check
Ball at feet: inside/outside rolls, v-pull, toe taps. Then 2-minute juggle check — players beat their Day 1 number. Celebrate anyone who has improved.
15–40
New Move of the Month (Differentiated)
Month 1: Step-Over — fake one direction, burst the other
Month 2: Cruyff Turn — fake cross, drag ball back, turn away
Month 3: Outside foot pass + overlap combination

Teach → slow reps → full speed → apply in 2v1

◉ Beginner◉ Advanced
PracticeStationary cone as defender, unlimited repsLive defender, full speed, finish on goal
GoalGet the motion rightUse it 3× in the scrimmage — they announce it when they do
Advanced players announcing when they successfully use the move in the scrimmage = accountability + pride. They'll work harder to pull it off.
40–55
Position Rotation Scrimmage
Put players in unfamiliar spots. Defenders play striker. Midfielders defend. Builds empathy, develops versatility, and keeps it fresh midseason. Normal small-sided game with full energy.
55–60
Cool-Down — Player-Led Stretch
Rotate who leads the stretch each session. Today's stretch leader picks a teammate to give a genuine compliment to. Builds leadership and culture simultaneously.
Game Day Pre-Game Warm-Up Routine ▾

Do This the Same Way Every Game

  • Routine = readiness. When the warm-up is the same, nerves settle and focus comes naturally.
  • Captain runs it. You observe and adjust only if needed.
−30
Arrive Together — Team Jog
2 laps of the field as a team. Together. Sets collective focus immediately.
−25
Dynamic Stretch (Team-Led)
High knees, butt kicks, hip circles, side shuffles, leg swings. Captain leads. 5 minutes. Coach is silent.
−20
Passing Pairs + Rondo
Quick passing pairs to get touches. Then 2-minute rondo to engage the mind. Eyes up, quick decisions.
−10
GK Warm-Up + Shape Walk-Through
GK takes 10 saves and 3 distributions. Outfield: one kickoff position walk-through and first defensive shape. Quick and calm.
−4
Pre-Game Talk + Prayer
2 tactical points max. "Today we _____ and _____." Captain speaks briefly. Coach prays — thank God, ask for safety, courage, unity. Then go.
Short talks win. Long pre-game speeches lose the room. Two clear points + prayer = locked in.
Build-Your-Own Practice

Drill Bank

A grab-and-go library for any week the pre-built sessions don't fit. Pick one warm-up, one technical block, one game, add a cool-down — done. Aim for roughly 60 minutes total.

How to Assemble a Session Fast

  • 5–10 min — Pick one Warm-Up
  • 15–20 min — Pick one or two Technical blocks (mix a beginner-friendly one with a more challenging one if you have time for both)
  • 20–25 min — Pick one Small-Sided Game
  • 5 min — Pick one Cool-Down
  • Short on time? Drop a Technical block before you drop the Game. The game is where it sticks.
  • Coming off a tough loss or feeling stale? Check Fitness/Conditioning — most of these double as competitive games, so intensity doesn't feel like punishment.
🏃 Warm-Ups 5–10 min each
10 min Dribble Maze ▾
Every player has a ball in a 20×20 grid. Coach calls commands: Red light (sole stop), Green light (dribble), Blue (inside turn), Yellow (outside chop). Gradually increase speed. Ends with 1 minute of freestyle — show your best move. Good early-season or as a refresher to check first-touch comfort.
10 min Cone Slalom + Juggling Station (Split) ▾
Half the team runs a timed slalom through 6 cones (20 yds), trying to beat their own time. Other half does a juggling count challenge. Swap after 5 minutes. Good for tracking individual improvement over the season.
10 min Pass-and-Move ▾
Two lines at midfield cones. Pass across, follow your pass to the opposite line. After 3 minutes, add: sprint to a wide cone after passing, then rejoin the opposite line. Simple, low-setup, gets bodies moving with a ball involved.
10 min Agility + Sprint Bursts ▾
Cone patterns or ladder drills: 2-feet-in, lateral shuffle, in-out. Then 4× 20 yd sprint at 80% effort with a recovery walk back. Use this before a high-tempo technical block or transition game — primes the legs for speed.
5–10 min Rondo 5v2 or 6v2 ▾
5 or 6 players keep the ball away from 2 defenders in a 12–15 yd circle. Two-touch max. Defenders earn a point for winning the ball in under 8 passes. High energy, gets everyone's head up and passing sharp right away — works as a warm-up at any point in the season.
5 min Team Juggling Challenge ▾
Everyone juggles at once. Drop the ball, sit down (but keep your own count going from memory). Last one standing gets nothing but pride. Quick way to loosen up a tired or tense team — works well after a loss or before a fun, light session.
⚙️ Technical Blocks 15–25 min each
20 min Passing Gates (Differentiated) ▾
Scatter 10 cone gates (2 cones, 2 yds apart) across the field. Pairs pass through as many gates as possible in 4 minutes, then try to beat their score.

Beginner: 5–8 yd passes, wide gates, unlimited touches, dominant foot fine.
Advanced: 10–15 yds, narrow gates, 2-touch max, must alternate feet each pass.
8 min First Touch Under Pressure (Differentiated) ▾
Pairs, 5 yds apart. Server passes firm — not soft — and receiver must control it away from their feet into open space before passing back.

Beginner: moderate rolling pass, 2 touches allowed.
Advanced: firm driven pass, 1 touch to control/set and 1 to pass, must check shoulder before the ball arrives.

Coaching cue: "Cushion it away from pressure, not back into it." Good recurring block all season — this skill never stops being worth reps.
20 min Move of the Day: Cut-Back Turn (Differentiated) ▾
Dribble toward a cone, chop the ball back with the inside foot, accelerate away. Teach → slow reps → full speed → line drill attacking a standing coach.

Beginner: slow approach, cone defender only, no finish required.
Advanced: sprint approach, live defender, turn + shot on goal.
20 min Shooting Circuit — 3 Stations (Differentiated) ▾
3 stations, 5–6 min each, rotate: ① Straight-on shot ② Rolling ball finish ③ Turn and shoot.

Beginner: closer range, stationary or slow feed, extra touch allowed before shooting.
Advanced: longer range, first-time finish required, no second touch.

Coaching cue: "Pick a corner — always have a target."
15–20 min Shadow Defending Footwork + 1v1 Delay Drill ▾
Pairs mirror defensive stance (low, balanced, goalside) for 5 min, then move to live 1v1: attacker starts 20 yds out, defender 5 yds away, job is to delay and force to the weak side — not win the ball immediately.

Beginner defender: success = stay in front for 5 seconds without lunging.
Advanced defender: success = force weak side AND win the ball cleanly.

Praise the patient defender loudly — lunging is the habit to break at this age.
15 min New Move of the Month (Differentiated) ▾
Rotate through a season-long menu: Step-Over (fake one way, burst the other), Cruyff Turn (fake cross, drag back, turn away), or outside-foot pass + overlap combo. Teach → slow reps → full speed → apply in a 2v1.

Beginner: stationary cone as defender, unlimited reps, goal is just getting the motion right.
Advanced: live defender, full speed, finish on goal — announce it out loud when they pull it off in the scrimmage.

Use on a week with 2+ rest days. Skip it on a heavy game week.
15 min Playing Out of the Back (Differentiated) ▾
Half-field. GK + 3 defenders build out against 2 high-press attackers. Goal: play the ball past midfield without booting it long.

Beginner: attackers press at half speed, GK can hold 5 seconds before distributing.
Advanced: full-speed press, must maintain possession through midfield, GK has 3 seconds to distribute.

Praise the brave short pass under pressure every time — that's the behavior that wins close games.
🥅 Set Pieces 10–20 min each
15 min Corner Kicks — 2 Plays ▾
Play 1 (Near-post flick): one runner attacks near post, everyone else creates space.
Play 2 (Short corner): ball to a player at the edge of the box, cross from a wider angle.

5 reps each, both sides, against a defensive wall for realistic pressure. Keep it to 2 plays — more than that gets forgotten.
12 min Free Kicks + Wall Practice ▾
Kicks from 20–25 yds. Teach wall setup (3–4 players, GK covers far post). Rotate who takes kicks — give beginners reps too. After 6 attempts, practice crossing free kicks with runners attacking.
5 min Throw-In Routine — 2 Options ▾
① Down the line — throw to a winger checking to the ball, who plays it forward into space.
② Flick-on — throw to a player with back to goal near midfield, who flicks it first-time into space behind the defense.

4 reps each, both sidelines. Both feet on the ground, ball behind the head, no foul throws. Two options only — don't overload the thrower's decision-making.
⚽ Small-Sided Games 10–20 min each
15–20 min 4v4 Passing Game (No Goals) ▾
30×20 yd grid. Score by passing through a cone gate — no shooting. Must complete 3 passes before scoring. Mix skill levels on each team. Advanced add-on: 5 passes in a row makes the next gate worth double, rewarding patience even from your strongest players.
10 min King of the Square (1v1) ▾
10×10 yd grids, pairs. Attacker dribbles to cross defender's end line; defender tries to knock the ball out. 45 seconds per round, winners rotate to new grids. Beginner tweak: defender can only shuffle side to side, no tackling, to build attacker confidence first.
10 min 3v1 Into Goal ▾
3 attackers vs 1 defender + GK, starting from midfield. Mix one advanced player with two beginners — the advanced player's job is to set teammates up, not score alone. Bonus point if a beginner scores. Great for teaching facilitation over solo play.
15 min 5v5 Defensive Points Game ▾
Normal 5v5, but award points beyond goals: 1 pt for a clean tackle, 2 pts for a GK save + distribution, 3 pts for a clean 2-minute defensive spell. Shifts the mindset from goals-only to total performance — good after a game where defending was the issue.
20 min Transition Game: Win It, Use It ▾
3v3 in a 25×20 grid, two small goals. When possession changes, the new team must get a shot within 6 seconds or possession resets. Beginner: 8-second window. Advanced: 4-second window, must switch direction before shooting. Teaches immediate forward thinking after a turnover.
15 min 3v2 Counterattack Waves ▾
3 attackers at midfield, 2 defenders at the 18. Coach passes, attackers go immediately. Wide players stay wide, ball carrier commits the defender, passes early. After each rep, 2 attackers become defenders for the next wave. Put beginners as wide runners for a clear, achievable role.
10 min 4v4 Dribble-to-Score ▾
Goals replaced by end lines — score by dribbling over the line, no shooting required. Rewards ball carriers taking players on. Good pairing with any dribbling-focused technical block.
15–20 min Position Rotation Scrimmage ▾
Normal small-sided game, but put players in unfamiliar spots — defenders play striker, midfielders defend. Builds versatility and empathy for teammates' roles, and keeps a midseason practice fresh.
Open Free Scrimmage ▾
No stoppages. Let them play. Best when you want to see natural tendencies, reward a specific fix in real time, or just need the team to enjoy soccer after a heavy technical stretch.
🔥 Fitness & Conditioning through the ball, not laps
10 min High-Intensity 3v3, 90-Second Rounds ▾
3v3, 90 seconds per round, no rest between rounds, winner stays on. Competitive pressure builds fitness without it feeling like a punishment. Good for a team that looked outworked in the last game.
Add-on Touch-Everyone Constraint ▾
Add to any scrimmage: a team can't score unless every outfield player has touched the ball during that possession. Raises movement and intensity without adding separate fitness work — use it if the team is jogging through a normal scrimmage.
8 min Sprint + Recover Pairs ▾
Pairs: one sprints 20 yds, turns, receives a pass, passes back. Repeat 5× each. Mimics the sprint/recover rhythm of a real game better than a flat-out conditioning run.
🧘 Cool-Downs 5 min each
5 min Shout-Out Circle ▾
Light stretch, then go around the circle: "Who made a teammate better today?" Call out a name. Builds the habit of noticing each other, not just results.
5 min Verse Response ▾
Read the week's verse. Ask: "What did you do today that matched this?" or "Where did you fall short?" Take 2–3 answers. Coach responds honestly too.
5 min Player-Led Stretch ▾
Rotate who leads the stretch each session. That player picks a teammate to give a genuine, specific compliment to. Builds leadership and culture at the same time.
1 min Silent 10 Seconds ▾
After a big win or a hard loss: "10 seconds of silence as a team." No words, just stillness. Signals that something bigger than the result just happened.
Position-Specific Training

Goalkeeper Development

Your goalkeeper touches the ball less than anyone but can decide more games than anyone. They need dedicated reps every session — not leftover attention at the end.

How to Run This

  • If you have an assistant coach or a parent volunteer comfortable with the basics, have them break off with your goalkeeper(s) for about 20 minutes per session while the rest of the team works on outfield drills.
  • If you're coaching solo, fold a shorter 10-minute GK block into the warm-up or technical window — see the "Solo Coach" note in each block below.
  • Rotate 2–3 players through GK over preseason, even if you know who your primary starter will be. Every team needs a backup who isn't seeing the position for the first time in a game.
  • This tab is a standing menu — pull blocks from it and slot them into the 20-minute GK window during any Week 1–3 or in-season session.
Core GK Blocks Rotate Through These All Season
Block 1 Footwork & Handling Basics ▾
~5
Footwork Warm-Up
Quick lateral shuffles between two cones 6 yds apart, staying low, hands ready. Add: coach calls "set" — keeper drops into ready stance (knees bent, hands out front, weight on toes). 5 sets of 20 seconds.
~10
Handling: W-Catches
Coach or partner throws/rolls balls at chest height from 8 yds. Keeper catches with hands forming a "W" behind the ball, pulls it into the chest immediately. 10 reps. Then add balls slightly to each side — keeper must move feet first, hands second.
The #1 youth keeper mistake is catching with the ball still out at arm's length instead of pulling it in. Say "secure it" every single rep until it's automatic.
~5
Low Ball Collection
Rolled balls along the ground from 10 yds. Keeper gets body behind the ball, scoops with both hands, one knee down on the ground behind the ball as a backstop. 8 reps, alternating sides.
Solo Coach: fold this into the team warm-up — pair the keeper with a partner doing the same rolling/throwing pattern for 10 minutes while everyone else dribbles.
Block 2 Diving & Shot-Stopping ▾
~5
Collapse Dive Technique (No Ball)
From the ready stance, practice the step-and-collapse motion to each side without a ball first — step toward the target with the near foot, drop the hip, land on the side (not the stomach or back). 6 reps each side, slow and controlled. Build the motor pattern before adding speed or a ball.
Don't rush to add a ball. A keeper who dives correctly without one will dive correctly with one. A keeper who never learns the mechanic will develop bad habits that are hard to undo later.
~10
Low Diving Saves
Coach rolls or gently kicks balls just out of reach to each side, 6–8 yds away. Keeper dives to collect using the technique above. 5 reps each side. Increase distance slightly as they improve — never increase speed and distance at the same time.
~5
Reaction Shots
From 12 yds, coach or a player strikes moderate-pace shots at the keeper — mix of low, high, and to both sides. Focus on getting body behind the ball and secure handling, not just blocking it away. 10 shots.
Solo Coach: this is the block worth a parent volunteer for, even just to roll/kick balls consistently. If truly solo, run it as a 10-minute station at the start of the shooting circuit (Day 3) while the rest of the team rotates through the other stations.
Block 3 Distribution ▾
~7
Throwing — Overarm & Bowl
Practice both: the overarm javelin-style throw for distance to a winger, and the low bowl/roll for a quick, accurate pass to a nearby defender restarting play. 8 reps each style, both to the left and right.
~7
Goal Kicks & Punts
10 goal kicks off the ground focusing on a consistent target (a teammate or zone, not just "far"). Then 10 punts out of hands, same focus on accuracy over distance. Praise a well-placed short kick to a teammate over a long one that goes straight out of bounds.
Middle school keepers often think distribution means "kick it as far as possible." Teach them early that a 15-yard pass to a teammate's feet beats a 40-yard kick that gives the ball straight back to the other team.
~6
Quick Distribution Under Pressure
Coach passes the ball back to the keeper like a back-pass. Keeper must control and distribute within 3 seconds to a target player making a run. Builds the habit of fast, decisive choices instead of panicking under pressure.
Solo Coach: tie this into the "Building Out of the Back" drill in Week 3, Day 7 — the keeper is already part of that drill, so the distribution reps come built in.
Block 4 Commanding Crosses & the Box ▾
~8
Catching Crosses
A server crosses balls in from the wing (start slow and easy, build up pace). Keeper practices timing the run, catching at the highest point, and calling "Keeper!" loudly before coming for it. 8–10 reps from both sides.
The call matters as much as the catch. A keeper who comes for a cross without calling for it is the #1 cause of collisions and own goals at this age. Drill the call every single time, even in practice.
~7
1v1 Breakaway Decision-Making
Attacker starts at midfield, dribbles in alone on goal. Keeper must decide: stay big and stand tall, or come out and close the angle/space. Run 6 reps. After each one, ask the keeper what they saw and why they chose that response.
~5
Communication with Defenders
During any 4v4 or 5v5 team drill, specifically coach the keeper to talk: "Step up," "I've got it," "Man on," "Time" (when a defender has time on the ball). This isn't a separate drill — it's a standing instruction to apply during every scrimmage all season.
Solo Coach: the 1v1 breakaway drill doubles as a finishing drill for outfield players — run it as a shared station so the keeper gets reps while strikers do too.

Season-Long GK Notes

  • Don't make the same player keeper every single practice. Rotating reps through 2–3 players keeps your backup ready and gives your primary keeper a break from getting shot at constantly.
  • A keeper learns more from 15 focused minutes than 45 unfocused ones. Quality of reps beats quantity — give clear feedback on every rep rather than just feeding balls continuously.
  • Praise communication and decision-making as much as the saves themselves. A keeper who organizes the defense well prevents more goals than one who just makes dramatic diving stops.
  • Build in 5 minutes for the keeper to also get outfield touches sometimes — rotate the GK position based on practice plan so no one player loses their feel for playing out.
Culture & Faith Framework

Building a Team That Lasts

Xs and Os are what you teach. Culture is what they remember. Here's how to build both intentionally as the Covenant Classical Warriors.

"No soldier gets entangled in civilian pursuits, since his aim is to please the one who enlisted him. An athlete is not crowned unless he competes according to the rules."
2 Timothy 2:4–5 — Season Anchor Verse
Culture Our 5 Team Values ▾
①
Discipline
Doing the right thing even when no one's watching and even when you don't feel like it — showing up on time, finishing every rep, holding your defensive shape when you're tired. Discipline in practice is what shows up in the 80th minute of a game.
②
Perseverance
Sticking with a hard drill, a losing scrimmage, or a frustrating skill until it clicks. No quitting on a rep, a teammate, or a game. Praise the player who keeps battling after a mistake more than the player who never makes one.
③
Sportsmanship
Play to win — competing hard matters. But winning isn't the ultimate goal. Preparation, effort, and attitude are what we control. Win with grace. Lose with grace. Shake every hand, every time, regardless of the score.
④
Self-Sacrifice
The pass instead of the shot. The wide run instead of the show. Playing out of position because the team needs it. Self-sacrifice on the field is practice for self-sacrifice everywhere else in life.
⑤
Teamwork
The most important value of all. Help your teammate, and the team is helped — and when the team is helped, every player on it is helped too. Assists matter as much as goals. We track and call out assists out loud, every game.
Culture Assists Over Glory — The Warriors Way ▾
🏆
Why "assists" is our word
Some of the most successful teams aren't the ones that score the most — they're the ones that lead in assists. Winning is the result of helping a teammate, not chasing individual glory. We are Warriors. Assists are how Warriors play.
→
How to coach it
Call out assists as loud as goals — in practice scrimmages and real games. Ask after every match: "Who had the best assist today?" before "Who scored?" Tell your most skilled players directly: your job is to make the player next to you better. That's the standard, not just scoring the most.
Culture Win Like a Warrior, Lose Like a Warrior ▾
🤝
The standard for both
We play to win — competing hard matters. But preparation, effort, and attitude — not the scoreboard — are what we actually control and what we're judged on. That means: shake the other team's hand the same way whether you won by five or lost by five. No celebrating in an opponent's face. No sulking, blaming, or excuse-making after a loss.
→
Use this after a tough loss
Sometimes a team that just got beat hasn't yet learned how to lose well — and sometimes a team that just won big hasn't learned how to win graciously either. Both are coachable moments. Name it plainly in the huddle: "We learn more from how we just handled that than from the final score." Then move to fixing one specific thing (see Session A in the In-Season tab).

Inclusion Managing Mixed Skill Levels Gracefully ▾
🔑
Never rank players publicly
Beginners know they're beginners. They don't need you to confirm it. Frame differentiated drills as "challenges" — the harder version is the advanced challenge, not the default. Every player is working on their own target.
🔑
Private correction, public praise
Correct technique privately (pull them aside or crouch down to their level). Praise publicly and specifically: not "good job" but "Marcus, that first touch was exactly right — you opened your body before the ball arrived."
🔑
Give beginners meaningful roles
In games: every beginner has a specific job (e.g., "your only job is to be wide and available"). In drills: give them a version they can succeed at. Success builds confidence — confidence builds skill. Shame does not.
🔑
This is mentorship, not just pairing
Coaches mentoring players is one of the most valuable parts of being on a team — and the same principle works player-to-player. When an advanced player partners with a beginner all season, frame it out loud as mentorship: "You're not just playing next to him, you're responsible for helping him get better." That language elevates it from a drill mechanic to a real leadership role they can take pride in.
🔑
Advanced players earn their status by serving
Tell your best players directly: "Your job in practice is to make your teammates better. That's how you earn the right to be the difference-maker in games." This changes the dynamic from individual showboating to team building.
🔑
Playing time philosophy
At this age and this school context: everyone plays meaningful minutes. Not necessarily equal — you still want to compete — but no one should feel invisible. Set a minimum per player per game and honor it. Communicate it to parents early.
Every player is made in the image of God and chosen for this team for a reason. They deserve to feel that.

Coach's Notes

Practical information you'll need throughout the season — formations, fitness, equipment, and halftime.

Tactics Formation & 3 Team Principles ▾
Form.
Recommended Formations (7v7)
1-2-3-1 — 2 defenders, 3 midfielders, 1 striker. Clear roles, easy to teach, good default for a mixed-ability team.

1-3-2-1 — 3 defenders, 2 midfielders, 1 striker. More defensively solid if you're facing a stronger team or want extra cover at the back; trade-off is less midfield possession.
Put your best athlete at central/defensive midfielder (not striker). In a 1-2-3-1 they touch the ball constantly and cover gaps on both sides — it's the highest-impact position for a mixed-ability team. In 7v7, one ball-winning, composed midfielder can cover for a lot of other mistakes.
①
Press high immediately after losing possession
The 3–4 seconds after you lose the ball is when you can win it back cheapest. Everyone presses toward the ball — not just the striker. With only 7 players, an organized press matters even more since there's less ground to cover behind it.
②
Play wide and cross
At this age, defenses collapse toward center. Wide channels are almost always open. Train your wide midfielders to receive and cross; train your striker to time runs into the box. Simple but effective against any team, and a 7v7 field's narrower width makes switching play side to side even more valuable.
③
Stay connected at the back
With only 2 defenders in a 1-2-3-1, there's no third center-back to bail you out. Both defenders shift together — when the ball goes wide, both slide that direction, never split apart. One defender getting isolated 1v1 in the box is the single biggest way 7v7 teams give up easy goals.
Format 7v7-Specific Notes ▾
→
Smaller field, faster game
7v7 fields are roughly a third the size of a full 11v11 pitch. Transitions happen fast — a turnover can become a shot on goal in 3–4 seconds. This is exactly why the transition and pressing drills in this plan matter so much; there's much less time to recover defensively than in a bigger format.
→
Every player covers more ground
With only 6 outfield players, there's no one to hide. A tired or disengaged player is much more exposed in 7v7 than in 11v11. Build in real water breaks and don't be afraid to rotate subs more often than you would in a bigger format — fresher legs matter more here.
→
Check your league's specific rules
Field size, offside rules (some 7v7 leagues don't call offside), build-out lines, and substitution rules vary a lot between 7v7 leagues. Confirm these with your league before the season so practice matches what actually happens on game day.
Fitness Conditioning Philosophy ▾
→
Condition through the ball, not laps
Intensive rondos, transition drills, and high-pressure small-sided games build soccer-specific fitness without boring running. 12–14 year olds disengage fast from pure cardio. Keep a ball involved at all times.
→
Watch for fatigue vs. effort
If players jog through a scrimmage, add a constraint: "Can't score unless every outfield player has touched the ball this possession." Raises intensity without adding physical work.
→
Pre-game legs
No intense training day before a game. The session before a game is for sharpness and confidence — not fitness gains. Overtrained 13-year-olds play flat.
→
Hydration reminder every session
Build in a water break around the 20-minute mark and at the transition to the game. Middle schoolers often don't self-regulate hydration well. It affects performance and mood.
Halftime Halftime Script ▾
1 min
Water, breathe, settle
Don't talk while they're still catching their breath. Let them drink and calm down first. You observe and organize your thoughts.
1 min
One thing we did well
Name it specifically. "Our press in the first 10 minutes was excellent." Not generic — specific. They need to know what to keep doing.
1 min
One adjustment
One thing only. "Their #7 keeps running in behind our left back. Left back, shift back 5 steps when the ball is on their right side." Specific, actionable, one player or one zone.
30 sec
Captain fires them up
Captain gets the last word. Coach steps back. Hands in. Go.
Total halftime: 3.5 minutes max. Long halftimes lose the room. Short, clear, and fired up wins every time at this age.
